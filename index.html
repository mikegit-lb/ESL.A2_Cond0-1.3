If This Happens… Conditional Sentences for Real Life

Level: A2 (Elementary / Pre-Intermediate)
Time: 75–90 Minutes
Grammar: Zero & First Conditional
Goal: Accurate real-life communication
Teacher Note: This structured framework provides complete immersion with zero placeholders. Keep the atmosphere dynamic! Focus heavily on the functional difference: Type 0 for absolute constants, Type 1 for predictive specific options.

Learning Objectives

By the end of this lesson, you will be able to:

  • Explain the fundamental difference between If Clause Type 0 and Type 1.
  • Use Type 0 accurately to express facts, rules, and daily routines.
  • Use Type 1 accurately to express real future possibilities and plans.
  • Form correct affirmative, negative, and question structures for conditionals.
  • Ask and answer conditional questions comfortably in real conversations.
  • Speak and write confidently about school, health, habits, weather, technology, and future intentions.

5. Grammar Explanation

Zero Conditional – Facts & General Truths

Meaning: We use the Zero Conditional to talk about things that are always true, scientific facts, automatic results, rules, or strong personal routines.

Form: If + Present Simple, Present Simple

Time Reference: General time (all time, past/present/future constants), not a specific singular moment.

Key Use Cases & Examples:

  • Scientific facts: If you freeze water, it turns into ice.
  • Daily routines: If I wake up early, I always go for a run.
  • Rules: If you drive a car, you need a valid license.
  • General human truths: If people eat too much sugar, they feel sick over time.

Common Pitfall to Avoid: Do not use "will" in a general fact statement.
Wrong: If you cross the street without looking, you will get hit. (If it's an absolute universal safety law of cause/effect, keep it simple present: Correct: ...you get hurt.)

First Conditional – Real Future Possibilities

Meaning: We use the First Conditional to talk about a realistic specific condition in the present or future, and its highly possible future result.

Form: If + Present Simple, will + base verb

Time Reference: A specific future point or specific upcoming event.

Key Use Cases & Examples:

  • Future plans: If it rains tomorrow, I will stay home.
  • Predictions: If I pass the exam, I will celebrate with my friends.
  • Warnings: If she studies tonight, she will do well on the test tomorrow.
  • Negotiations/Promises: If you wash the dishes, I will cook dinner.

Common Pitfall to Avoid: Never put "will" directly inside the if-clause!
Wrong: If I will see him tomorrow, I will tell him.
Correct: If I see him tomorrow, I will tell him.

6. Zero vs First Conditional Comparison Table

Study these 8 side-by-side examples to master the situational difference between a general rule (Type 0) and a single future event (Type 1).

Grammar Type Meaning / Time Focus Structural Form Example Sentence What it actually means to the student Common Mistake to Watch
Zero Conditional General Routine (Anytime) Present + Present If I drink coffee at night, I don't sleep. This usually/always happens to me as a rule. If I drink coffee, I won't sleep. (when meaning always)
First Conditional Specific Future (Tonight) Present + Will + Verb If I drink coffee tonight, I won't sleep. This is a real risk for this specific upcoming night. If I will drink coffee tonight...
Zero Conditional Scientific Fact Present + Present If you heat ice, it melts. An unchangeable law of nature. Always true. If you heat ice, it will melts.
First Conditional Specific Prediction Present + Will + Verb If you leave that ice cream outside, it will melt fast. A warning about a specific event happening soon. If you leave it, it melts fast tomorrow.
Zero Conditional School Rule / Habit Present + Present If students miss class, they fail the participation mark. Standard official school operating regulation. If students miss class, they will fail always.
First Conditional Specific Warning Present + Will + Verb If you miss class tomorrow, you will miss the final test. A specific immediate warning for tomorrow's instance. If you will miss class tomorrow...
Zero Conditional Emotional Habit Present + Present If I feel lonely, I call my mother. This is my automatic psychological habit. If I feel lonely, I will call my mom usually.
First Conditional Specific Future Plan Present + Will + Verb If I feel lonely later, I will call my mother. A planned action for an upcoming moment today. If I will feel lonely later...

7. Form Table & Clause Order Rules

You can change the order of the clauses without changing the core meaning. Pay close attention to the comma rules!

Zero Conditional Structure

Clause Order Structural Layout Example Sentence
If-Clause First (Comma needed) If + Present Simple , Present Simple If it rains, I stay inside.
Result Clause First (No comma) Present Simple + if + Present Simple I stay inside if it rains.

First Conditional Structure

Clause Order Structural Layout Example Sentence
If-Clause First (Comma needed) If + Present Simple , will + base verb If it rains tomorrow, I will stay inside.
Result Clause First (No comma) will + base verb + if + Present Simple I will stay inside if it rains tomorrow.
💡 Quick Rule Reminder: Think of the word "If" as a heavy anchor. When it comes at the very beginning of a sentence, you must place a comma after that complete idea to take a breath before the result. When "if" sits in the middle, it connects the sentences perfectly on its own—so no comma is required!

8. Fix These Common Mistakes!

Look carefully at how to fix these 12 common mistakes made by students at the A2 level. Understand why each change is necessary.

  1. Wrong: If it will rain tomorrow, I will stay home.
    Correct: If it rains tomorrow, I will stay home.
    Explanation: Never put will in the conditional "if" part of the sentence.

  2. Wrong: If I heat ice, it will melts.
    Correct: If I heat ice, it melts.
    Explanation: This is a universal fact, so use the regular Present Simple for Type 0. Also, "will" must always be followed by a bare verb without "s".

  3. Wrong: If I am hungry, I will eats.
    Correct: If I am hungry, I will eat.
    Explanation: After the modal verb "will", you must use the bare base form of the verb without adding an "s".

  4. Wrong: If people don’t sleep, they will tired.
    Correct: If people don’t sleep, they get tired / are tired.
    Explanation: "Tired" is an adjective, not a verb! You need an active verb like "get" or "are" to make a complete predicate.

  5. Wrong: If she don't study, she fails.
    Correct: If she doesn't study, she fails.
    Explanation: Remember subject-verb agreement in Present Simple. "She" requires the singular helper "doesn't".

  6. Wrong: If I will be free, I call you.
    Correct: If I am free, I will call you.
    Explanation: Swap the structural components. Present simple belongs in the if-clause, and future prediction goes in the result.

  7. Wrong: I will visit you if I will have time.
    Correct: I will visit you if I have time.
    Explanation: Even if the if-clause is at the end of the sentence, it still rejects "will".

  8. Wrong: If you touch fire you get burned.
    Correct: If you touch fire, you get burned.
    Explanation: Missing comma! You must separate an initial if-clause from the rest of the sentence.

  9. Wrong: If I see her tomorrow I tell her.
    Correct: If I see her tomorrow, I will tell her.
    Explanation: For a specific real event happening tomorrow, the result clause requires "will".

  10. Wrong: If you mix yellow and blue, you will got green.
    Correct: If you mix yellow and blue, you get green.
    Explanation: For Type 0 facts, use the present simple base form, never a past tense form like "got".

  11. Wrong: If the phone rings, I answer it always.
    Correct: If the phone rings, I always answer it.
    Explanation: Frequency adverbs like "always" should sit directly before the main action verb.

  12. Wrong: If he comes here, we have a great party tomorrow.
    Correct: If he comes here, we will have a great party tomorrow.
    Explanation: The word "tomorrow" signals a single future possibility, requiring Type 1 structure.

Each sentence below contains exactly one conditional grammar error. Identify and fix it!

  1. If I will be tired, I always drink hot tea.

    If I am tired, I always drink hot tea.
    Explanation: General routine (Type 0) requires Present Simple in the if-clause, never will.

  2. If it rains tomorrow, we stay at home.

    I will be very happy if I pass the exam on Friday.

  7. If you cross an ocean by ship you see lots of water.

    If you cross an ocean by ship, you see lots of water. (Missing comma)

  8. If my father works late, he will always call our home phone.

    If my father works late, he always calls our home phone.

  9. If they will don't leave early, they will miss the flight.

    If they don't leave early, they will miss the flight.

15. Reading Practice

A Healthy and Smart Week

My name is Alex, and I have a very simple plan for my life routine. If I wake up early on weekdays, I always feel energetic all day. Every single morning, I follow a strict rule: if I drink a full glass of water right after waking up, my body starts working beautifully. I never skip breakfast because if I skip my first morning meal, I lose my focus in school classes completely.

This upcoming week is very special because I am preparing for my final computer science test. If I study hard every evening this week, I will pass the exam easily on Friday morning. My school teacher always tells us that if a student pays attention to the lessons, their school grades improve naturally.

I am also looking at the weekend environment weather reports. If the hot sun shines bright on Saturday morning, I will ride my bicycle around the city lake. But my friend told me that the weather can change quickly. If it pours with heavy rain on Saturday, we will visit the historical city museum instead. Later on Sunday, if my phone battery dies during my outdoor sightseeing walks, I will use my old paper map to find the way back home. I am sure that if I manage my time well this weekend, I will have a wonderful and productive holiday!

Part A: True or False

  1. Alex feels tired all day if he wakes up early on weekdays. (True / False) - Ans: False
  2. Alex believes that skipping breakfast causes him to lose focus in school. (True / False) - Ans: True
  3. The computer science test takes place on Friday morning. (True / False) - Ans: True
  4. Alex will visit the city museum if it is sunny on Saturday. (True / False) - Ans: False
  5. Alex plans to use an old paper map if his phone runs out of power. (True / False) - Ans: True
  6. His teacher thinks paying attention doesn't change school outcomes. (True / False) - Ans: False
  7. Alex drinks water immediately after waking up as a morning habit. (True / False) - Ans: True
  8. Alex expects a terrible weekend if he manages his time poorly. (True / False) - Ans: True

Part B: Comprehension Questions

  1. What does Alex do immediately after waking up every single morning? (Ans: He drinks a full glass of water.)
  2. What special event is Alex preparing for throughout this week? (Ans: His final computer science test.)
  3. What will Alex do on Saturday if the weather remains sunny and nice? (Ans: He will ride his bicycle around the city lake.)
  4. What is his backup plan if it pours with heavy rain on Saturday? (Ans: He will visit the historical city museum with his friend.)
  5. How does Alex's body react generally if he skips his morning breakfast meal? (Ans: He completely loses his ability to focus in his school classes.)
  6. According to his teacher, what happens to students who pay active attention? (Ans: Their school grades improve naturally.)
  7. What tool will Alex rely on if his high-tech smartphone device goes completely offline or dies? (Ans: An old paper map.)
  8. What final condition determines whether Alex enjoys a wonderful and productive holiday? (Ans: If he manages his time well.)

Part C: Grammar Noticing Challenge

23. Clear Board Plan Reference Layout

[GRAMMAR BOARD OUTLINE - TYPE 0 vs TYPE 1]

------------------------------------------------------------------------

ZERO CONDITIONAL (Type 0) -> ALWAYS TRUE / FACT / HABIT ROUTINE

Structure: IF + Present Simple [ , ] Present Simple

Example 1: If you heat clean ice, it melts automatically.

Example 2: If I look directly at the bright sun, my eyes hurt.

------------------------------------------------------------------------

FIRST CONDITIONAL (Type 1) -> FUTURE POSSIBILITY / PLANNED RISK

Structure: IF + Present Simple [ , ] WILL + Base Verb form

Example 1: If it pours with rain tomorrow morning, I will stay inside.

Example 2: If she passes her final exam on Friday, she will celebrate.

------------------------------------------------------------------------

CRITICAL WARNING MISTAKE RULE: NEVER PLACE "WILL" INSIDE THE IF CLAUSE SCRIPT!

* If it will rain tomorrow, I will stay inside. (WRONG COPIES)

* If it rains tomorrow, I will stay inside. (CORRECT MATCH)
